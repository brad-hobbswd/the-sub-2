Chapter 8

The Stories We Believe

Every person carries stories about who they are, what they are worth, and what they should expect from life. Some of those stories inspire growth and hope. Others quietly shape decisions, relationships, and struggles in ways that often go unnoticed for years.

"The stories we believe about ourselves often become the lives we live."

Long before people make decisions, they develop beliefs. Long before behaviors become habits, stories begin taking shape within the heart and mind. These stories are not always spoken aloud, and many people are not even fully aware of them. Yet they influence the way life is interpreted, the way relationships are experienced, and the way individuals see themselves every day.

Some stories are healthy.

A person who believes they have value often approaches life differently than someone who believes they do not. A person who believes mistakes can become opportunities for growth usually responds differently than someone who views every failure as proof of inadequacy. The stories people carry influence far more than they realize.

Many of those stories begin forming early in life.

Experiences teach lessons. Relationships communicate messages. Successes and disappointments leave impressions. Encouragement builds confidence. Rejection creates doubts. Acceptance fosters belonging. Criticism sometimes creates insecurity.

Over time, those experiences begin forming conclusions about identity, worth, safety, trust, and purpose.

A child who repeatedly feels unseen may begin believing their needs do not matter.

A teenager who experiences rejection may begin believing they will never truly belong.

Someone who faces repeated failure may begin believing they are incapable of success.

A person who experiences betrayal may begin believing vulnerability is dangerous.

The original experiences may pass, yet the stories often remain.

What makes these stories so powerful is that people frequently stop questioning them. The beliefs become familiar. Familiarity creates acceptance. Eventually, assumptions begin feeling like facts.

This process can have a profound influence on addiction.

Imagine someone carrying the belief that they are fundamentally broken. Every mistake reinforces the story. Every disappointment strengthens the narrative. Every relapse appears to confirm what they already suspect about themselves.

Now imagine someone carrying the belief that they are unworthy of love. Relationships become difficult because acceptance feels temporary. Compliments are dismissed. Kindness creates suspicion. The individual continues searching for something capable of filling a void that no substitute can truly satisfy.

These examples reveal an important truth. Addiction does not exist separately from a person's beliefs. Addiction often becomes intertwined with the stories people tell themselves about who they are and what they deserve.

The substitute may provide temporary relief from those stories, but relief is not the same as transformation.

For a season, the substitute quiets the internal voice. The doubts become softer. The insecurities become less noticeable. The painful beliefs fade into the background. Yet once the substitute disappears, the stories remain exactly where they were before.

This reality explains why recovery often involves much more than ending a behavior. Recovery frequently requires challenging beliefs that have existed for years.

The person who believes they are worthless must learn to see themselves differently.

The person who believes they are beyond hope must learn to imagine a different future.

The person who believes failure defines them must discover that failure and identity are not the same thing.

Such change rarely happens overnight. Deeply rooted beliefs are not easily replaced because they have often been reinforced through years of experience. Yet healing becomes possible when those stories are brought into the light and examined honestly.

One of the most important questions a person can ask is this:

Is the story true?

Many beliefs survive simply because they have never been challenged. They are accepted without examination. They influence decisions without being questioned. The moment those stories are explored honestly, their power often begins weakening.

The process can feel uncomfortable because old beliefs frequently provide a strange sense of familiarity. Even painful stories can feel safer than uncertainty. Letting go of a false identity often requires courage because it means stepping into something new.

As I reflected on the stories people carry, another realization emerged. The stories we believe eventually shape the way we see ourselves. Over time, beliefs become identity. Identity becomes the lens through which life is experienced.

This connection may explain why addiction can become so deeply attached to a person's sense of self.

The struggle is no longer limited to behavior.

The struggle becomes personal.

The individual begins asking questions that reach far beyond addiction.

Who am I?

What is my value?

Can I become something different than I have been?

Those questions lead directly into one of the most important conversations in the healing journey.

The conversation about identity.
