Introduction

The Heart Behind the Addiction

The first book explored the possibility that addiction often functions as a substitute for something deeper. This book turns its attention toward the person behind the addiction and asks a new question. What is happening inside the heart of the person who needs the substitute?

"Behind every addiction is a person. Behind every person is a story. Within every story is a heart searching for something."

The first book began with a question that stayed with me long after many conversations about addiction had ended.

What is the addiction substituting for?

That question led me into a journey that extended far beyond substances, compulsive behaviors, and recovery programs. As I listened to stories, reflected on personal experiences, participated in recovery communities, and continued searching for understanding, I became increasingly convinced that addiction cannot be fully understood by focusing only on the addiction itself. Beneath the visible struggle often exists a deeper story, one that involves emotional wounds, unmet needs, fears, longings, disappointments, and burdens that may have been present long before the substance or behavior ever appeared.

Throughout The Substitute, we explored the possibility that addiction often functions as a substitute for something deeper. Relief from anxiety, escape from emotional pain, freedom from loneliness, acceptance after rejection, comfort during grief, and peace amid inner chaos all became part of the discussion. The more I explored those themes, the more I realized that every substitute points toward a need, and every need points toward a person.

That realization eventually led me to another question.

What is happening inside the heart of the person who needs the substitute?

The question may seem simple at first, yet it has the potential to change the way we think about addiction entirely. Many conversations about addiction focus primarily on behavior. We talk about substances, consequences, relapses, broken promises, and recovery plans. Those conversations matter because addiction creates real damage in the lives of individuals, families, and communities. At the same time, behavior alone rarely explains the deeper struggle.

Behind every addiction is a human being.

Behind every human being is a story.

Within every story are experiences that shape how a person understands themselves and the world around them. Some stories contain rejection. Others contain loneliness, fear, trauma, grief, shame, disappointment, or emotional wounds that never fully healed. Some individuals carry burdens so quietly that even those closest to them never realize the weight they have been carrying for years.

Addiction does not erase those realities. In many cases, addiction develops within them.

This book is not an attempt to excuse addiction or remove personal responsibility. Accountability remains essential. Recovery requires honesty, courage, effort, and change. Yet accountability and understanding do not stand in opposition to one another. In fact, meaningful understanding often becomes one of the most important tools available in the healing process.

When we begin looking beneath the behavior, we discover that many people are fighting battles that remain largely invisible. We discover fears that rarely find words. We discover shame that shapes identity. We discover loneliness that exists even in crowded rooms. We discover people who desperately want relief from pain they do not know how to carry. We discover hearts searching for peace while reaching toward substitutes that promise far more than they can deliver.

The chapters that follow explore that hidden world.

Together we will examine emotional wounds, shame, fear, loneliness, identity, self worth, hope, recovery, healing, and the deeper longings that often exist beneath addiction. Rather than focusing exclusively on the substitute, we will focus on the person who needs the substitute. We will explore what happens inside the heart long before addiction appears and what must often happen inside the heart if lasting healing is to occur.

My hope is that this journey will help family members gain a deeper understanding of those they love. My hope is that those who struggle personally will discover language for experiences they have carried for years without fully understanding them. Most importantly, my hope is that every reader will begin seeing addiction through a lens of both truth and compassion.

The first book asked what the addiction is substituting for.

This book asks a different question.

What is happening inside the heart of the person who needs the substitute?

The answer to that question may reveal more than we ever expected to find.
